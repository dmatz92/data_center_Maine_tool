These are the main permitting hurdles, in plain terms. Most are triggered automatically by a facility this size; a few depend on the specific site.

How to read this. What a facility uses and what it provides are shown side by side at the size you choose. The bars show how steeply each item changes with size—items scale differently, with some rising in step with size and others changing little. No single "score" is shown.

Sources & limits. Power and water from facility-efficiency ranges (WUE/PUE, Lawrence Berkeley); Maine electricity use ~11,300 GWh/yr and sales tax 5.5% (EIA, Maine Revenue Services); Maine residential consumption 6.6 MWh/household/year (EIA 2024, 550 kWh/month average \u2014 roughly half the national average because most Maine homes heat with oil or propane rather than electricity); equipment exemption per Maine's BETE program; build cost from 2025–26 industry benchmarks. Job and revenue multipliers are placeholders pending an IMPLAN or REMI model calibrated to Maine. Employment-effect estimates informed by Brookings Institution (2026), which found 4–5% county employment growth at hyperscale facilities using synthetic control methods — and found naive estimates (common in developer-commissioned studies) overstate effects by ~3x. Ratepayer risk is qualitative; the mechanism (infrastructure costs entering the rate base under cost-of-service regulation) applies at every size — what scales with facility size is the magnitude of investment that could be socialized, not whether the mechanism exists. Maine has no statewide large-load cost-allocation rule as of this writing. LBNL (2019–2025 analysis) found data centers were not a major driver of retail electricity price increases nationally during that period, but that predates the current AI-driven buildout and reflects national averages rather than Maine-specific grid conditions.

Not legal, engineering, or financial advice. For council discussion; verify against primary Maine statutes and a site-specific study before any decision.
