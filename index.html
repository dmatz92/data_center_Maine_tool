Assumptions & sources

Site Law (SLODA): The controlling trigger for a data center is the “structure” threshold—buildings, parking, and paved/graded area over 3 acres (Title 38 M.R.S. §482). Projects of 3–10 acres may be delegated to a municipality registered with DEP. The 20-acre figure is the separate general land-area trigger.

Air: Maine licenses minor sources under Chapter 115 and major (Title V) sources under Chapter 140. Backup generators are typically engineered with fuel/hour caps to stay “synthetic minor” below Clean Air Act major-source thresholds (100/250 tpy)—as in the Nordic Aquafarms application.

Water & discharge: MEPDES wastewater license (DEP, delegated NPDES) applies to any discharge to waters of the State; evaporative blowdown is the usual trigger. Withdrawal review depends on source and volume.

NRPA / §404: Any wetland, stream, great-pond, or shoreland disturbance triggers Natural Resources Protection Act review (DEP) plus federal Section 404 (US Army Corps).

Grid: Interconnection study with ISO-NE and the serving utility; large loads increasingly face dedicated study (FERC ANOPR, Oct 2025).

Assumptions & sources

Water (WUE): Liters of water per kWh of IT energy. Archetype ranges: air-cooled ~0.0–0.2; closed-loop ~0.2–0.5; evaporative ~1.8–1.9 (U.S. average, Lawrence Berkeley 2016). At ~1.8 L/kWh a 100 MW facility has been estimated near 1.1M gallons/day.

Energy (PUE): Total facility energy = IT energy × PUE. Efficient designs ~1.1; air-cooled higher. Annual energy = MW × 8,760 hours.

Trade-off: Lower-water cooling means higher energy use—the water and grid figures move in opposite directions by design.

Grid: Magnitude flag only. Whether a specific substation can host the load, and upgrade cost, require a site-specific interconnection study. Historically a large majority of interconnection-queue entrants withdraw before connecting (Lawrence Berkeley).

Read these separately. Construction jobs and one-time sales/use tax are temporary—they stop once the build ends. The recurring figure (chiefly annual property tax) is the decision-relevant one for whether a project pays off over time, and it is precisely what incentives like Maine’s Business Equipment Tax Exemption (BETE) can erase. Permanent employment is modest; independent research finds data-center incentives have limited measurable effect on local jobs.
Assumptions & sources

Capital cost: Standard/cloud ~$9–12M per MW; AI-ready ~$20M per MW (2025–26 industry benchmarks). Shown figure is the construction-and-infrastructure portion, separate from imported IT/server cost.

One-time sales/use tax: Maine’s general sales/use rate is 5.5% statewide (no local sales tax). Applied here to the taxable-materials share of construction spend (assumed ~40%; labor is not taxed). If the equipment exemption applies, most machinery/equipment purchases are sales-tax exempt, sharply reducing this figure.

Annual property tax: Estimated as assessed value × mill rate. Assessment ratio assumed ~70% of capital value; mill rate range ~12–20 per $1,000 (rural to urban Maine). Maine’s Business Equipment Tax Exemption (BETE) exempts most business equipment from property tax—and a data center’s value is overwhelmingly equipment—so the exemption toggle dramatically changes this line.

Why the toggle matters: Because a data center’s assessed value is mostly equipment, BETE-style exemptions can move recurring property-tax revenue close to zero. This is the central fiscal trade-off the council should weigh; the toggle lets you see both ends.

Net-fiscal & jobs: Multipliers are placeholders pending IMPLAN/REMI selection and Maine calibration. Worked templates: Maryland (IMPLAN), New York/Micron (REMI, reported ~2.0 fiscal benefit-cost ratio).
